dominant Internet search provider reflects a generationally-inflected notion that <i>everything</i> that matters is now on the Web, and <i>should</i>, in the moral sense of the verb, be accessible through search. In this theoretically nuanced study of search technology&#8217;s broader implications for knowledge production and social relations, the authors shed light on a culture of search in which our increasing reliance on search engines influences not only the way we navigate, classify, and evaluate Web content, but also how we think about ourselves and the world around us, online and off. </p> <p>Ken Hillis, Michael Petit, and Kylie Jarrett seek to understand the ascendancy of search and its naturalization by historicizing and contextualizing Google&#8217;s dominance of the search industry, and suggest that the contemporary culture of search is inextricably bound up with a metaphysical longing to manage, order, and categorize all knowledge.

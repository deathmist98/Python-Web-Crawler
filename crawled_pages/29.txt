initially, in testing \r\n  beginning August 29, a request for Google led to the error &quot;host not found,&quot; \r\n  consistent with requests for other inaccessible or blocked sites. However, using \r\n  related methods, the authors have now confirmed and documented reports that \r\n  Chinese Internet access currently provides pages other than the ordinary Google \r\n  home page in response to requests for google.com; such behavior is believed \r\n  to have begun on September 8. The screen shots below document 6 instances of \r\n  this replacement.</font></p>\r\n<p><font face="ariel, sans-serif" font size="-1"><i>Update (11:00 PM EST on September \r\n  11): Sources in China and the <a\r\nhref="/filtering/china/test">Real-Time Testing System</a> confirm that Google \r\n  is now reachable in China as usual. Requests for www.google.com now yield the \r\n  expected Google search page (and not the third-party sites preserved below). \r\n  However, searches for certain controversial sites appear to be restricted. The \r\n  authors are working to prepare a sample of specific prohibited terms as well \r\n  as to document the method of filtering. </i></font></p>\r\n<blockquote>\r\n  <p><font face="ariel, sans-serif" font size="-1"><i>Current testing suggests \r\n    that filtering may vary across China. Some parts of China may not filter Google \r\n    keywords at all, while other parts may disconnect a user\'s Internet connection \r\n    (even an &quot;always-on&quot; high-speed connection) in response to a search \r\n    for a prohibited term. Prohibited terms seem to include &quot;jiang zemin&quot; \r\n    and &quot;cache&quot;; the latter is typically associated with requests to \r\n    use Google\'s system for viewing archived content from other web sites.</i></font></p>\r\n</blockquote>\r\n<font face="ariel, sans-serif" font size="-1">\r\n<p>&nbsp;</p>\r\n<p align="left"><font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif"><b><font size="-1"><a name="meth"></a>Methodology</font></b></font></p>\r\n<p><font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="-1">The authors connected to \r\n  the Chinese network so as to simulate network access just as experienced by \r\n  ordinary users located in Beijing, China.

class=\'part-title\'><p><small>Chapter 4: Web Search</small></p></div>\t\t\t<section data-type="chapter" class="standard post-77 chapter type-chapter status-publish hentry">\n<header>\n\t<h1 class="entry-title">\n\t<span>15</span> 4.1 Search Engines\t</h1>\n\t\t</header>\n<p>From\xc2\xa0https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine</p>\n<p><span>A </span><b>web search engine</b><span> is a software system that is designed to search for information on the </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a><span>. The search results are generally presented in a line of results often referred to as </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_engine_results_page" title="Search engine results page">search engine results pages</a><span> (SERPs). The information may be a mix of </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web pages</a><span>, images, and other types of files. Some search engines also\xc2\xa0</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">mine data</a><span> available in </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a><span> or </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_directory" title="Web directory">open directories</a><span>. Unlike </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_directories" class="mw-redirect" title="Web directories">web directories</a><span>, which are maintained only by human editors, search engines also maintain </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-time_computing" title="Real-time computing">real-time</a><span> information by running an </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a><span> on a </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a><span>.</span></p>\n<p>Modern Web Browser History</p>\n<p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JumpStation" title="JumpStation">JumpStation</a> (created in December 1993<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathon_Fletcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Jonathon Fletcher">Jonathon Fletcher</a>) used a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web robot</a> to find web pages and to build its index, and used a\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Web form">web form</a> as the interface to its query program. It was thus the first WWW resource-discovery tool to combine the three essential features of a web search engine (crawling, indexing, and searching) as described below. Because of the limited resources available on the platform it ran on, its indexing and hence searching were limited to the titles and headings found in the web pages the crawler encountered.</p>\n<p>One of the first &#8220;all text&#8221; crawler-based search engines was\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WebCrawler" title="WebCrawler">WebCrawler</a>, which came out in 1994. Unlike its predecessors, it allowed users to search for any word in any webpage, which has become the standard for all major search engines since. It was also the first one widely known by the public. Also in 1994, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lycos" title="Lycos">Lycos</a> (which started at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>) was launched and became a major commercial endeavor.</p>\n<p>Soon after, many search engines appeared and vied for popularity. These included <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magellan_(search_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magellan (search engine)">Magellan</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excite" title="Excite">Excite</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infoseek" title="Infoseek">Infoseek</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inktomi_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inktomi (company)">Inktomi</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Light_Group" title="Northern Light Group">Northern Light</a>, and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AltaVista" title="AltaVista">AltaVista</a>. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> was among the most popular ways for people to find web pages of interest, but its search function operated on its\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_directory" title="Web directory">web directory</a>, rather than its full-text copies of web pages. Information seekers could also browse the directory instead of doing a keyword-based search.</p>\n<p>In 1996, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a> was looking to give a single search engine an exclusive deal as the featured search engine on Netscape&#8217;s web browser. There was so much interest that instead Netscape struck deals with five of the major search engines: for $5 million a year, each search engine would be in rotation on the Netscape search engine page. The five engines were Yahoo!, Magellan, Lycos, Infoseek, and Excite.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> adopted the idea of selling search terms in 1998, from a small search engine company named <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goto.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Goto.com">goto.com</a>. This move had a significant effect on the SE business, which went from struggling to one of the most profitable businesses in the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Search engines were also known as some of the brightest stars in the Internet investing frenzy that occurred in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Several companies entered the market spectacularly, receiving record gains during their <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offerings</a>. Some have taken down their public search engine, and are marketing enterprise-only editions, such as Northern Light. Many search engine companies were caught up in the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, a speculation-driven market boom that peaked in 1999 and ended in 2001.</p>\n<p>Around 2000, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google&#8217;s search engine</a> rose to prominence.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The company achieved better results for many searches with an innovation called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PageRank" title="PageRank">PageRank</a>, as was explained in the paper <i>Anatomy of a Search Engine</i> written by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergey_Brin" title="Sergey Brin">Sergey Brin</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a>, the later founders of Google.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterative_algorithm" class="mw-redirect" title="Iterative algorithm">iterative algorithm</a> ranks web pages based on the number and PageRank of other web sites and pages that link there, on the premise that good or desirable pages are linked to more than others. Google also maintained a minimalist interface to its search engine. In contrast, many of its competitors embedded a search engine in a\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">web portal</a>. In fact, Google search engine became so popular that spoof engines emerged such as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mystery_Seeker" title="Mystery Seeker">Mystery Seeker</a>.</p>\n<p>By 2000, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> was providing search services based on Inktomi&#8217;s search engine. Yahoo! acquired Inktomi in 2002, and\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">Overture</a> (which owned <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlltheWeb" title="AlltheWeb">AlltheWeb</a> and AltaVista) in 2003. Yahoo! switched to Google&#8217;s search engine until 2004, when it launched its own search engine based on the combined technologies of its acquisitions.</p>\n<p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> first launched MSN Search in the fall of 1998 using search results from Inktomi. In early 1999 the site began to display listings from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Looksmart" class="mw-redirect" title="Looksmart">Looksmart</a>, blended with results from Inktomi. For a short time in 1999, MSN Search used results from AltaVista instead. In 2004, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> began a transition to its own search technology, powered by its own <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a> (called\xc2\xa0<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Msnbot" title="Msnbot">msnbot</a>).</p>\n<p>Microsoft&#8217;s rebranded search engine, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bing" title="Bing">Bing</a>, was launched on June 1, 2009. On July 29, 2009, Yahoo! and Microsoft finalized a deal in which <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo! Search</a> would be powered by Microsoft Bing technology.</p>\n</section>\n</div><!-- #content -->\n\t\t\t\t\t<nav class="nav-reading " role="navigation">\n\t\t<div class="nav-reading__previous
